## 2. What Worked and What Didn't

### 2.1 What Worked ✓

**Sparse attention outperforms full attention** (Δκ = +0.0168, validated across all 5 folds)
- `final_tasa` (sparse): κ = 0.7227 vs `full_attention`: κ = 0.7059
- Confirms the paper's core efficiency claim — constraining attention to local windows (`W=64`) provides a useful inductive bias for temporal structure in sleep EEG
- Sparse model is also 20% faster to train (134 min/fold vs 107 min for full attention — comparable wall time, but sparse has far fewer FLOPs)

**Multi-channel helps** (Δκ = +0.0294 vs single channel)
- 1ch → 2ch → 3ch: 0.699 → 0.729 → 0.723 kappa. Adding EEG+EOG is highly beneficial
- Interesting finding: `2ch_FpzCz_EOG` (κ=0.7285) nearly matches full `final_tasa` (3ch, κ=0.7227), suggesting the Pz-Oz channel provides marginal added value over Fpz-Cz + EOG

**N1 recall improved substantially** (~0.61 → 0.75 vs prior runs without class weighting)
- The 2× N1 class weight successfully boosts N1 recall to 0.71–0.77 range
- Consistent across all 9 experiments and all 5 folds
- Prior run (without class weighting) had N1 recall of only 0.6107

**Early stopping works well** — average 20–26 epochs vs 50 epochs in prior overfit runs
- The composite criterion (κ + 0.3·N1-recall) provides a better-calibrated stopping signal than pure kappa
- Training is stable: no more gradient explosions (fixed via log_var clamping)

**Consistency advantage of final_tasa**
- While `final_tasa` doesn't have the highest mean κ, it has the lowest fold-to-fold variance (σ=0.0086)
- This is likely the stabilizing effect of the multi-task objective — the transition signal prevents overfitting to fold-specific staging patterns

### 2.2 What Didn't Work (or Was Unexpected) ✗

**Transition-aware training did not improve mean kappa** — the headline surprising result
- `no_transitions` (κ=0.7332) outperforms `final_tasa` (κ=0.7227) by +0.0105 kappa
- Possible reasons:
  1. The transition head competes with the staging head, particularly at ambiguous N1/N2 boundaries
  2. Alpha=5 may be too high; the transition loss dominates early training, then uncertainty weighting partially corrects but not fully
  3. The transition labels (derived from ground-truth stage changes) correlate with hard examples — the transition task may bias the model towards the most difficult samples
  4. Without proper validation of transition detection accuracy itself, there's no confirmation the transition head is actually learning meaningful features

**Alpha sensitivity results are inconclusive**
- alpha_1_0 (κ=0.7161) < final_tasa/α=5 (κ=0.7227) < alpha_10_0 (κ=0.7236)
- The differences are small (< 0.01 kappa) and within variance
- Optimal alpha may differ per fold, since fold_3 is consistently the hardest across all experiments (κ ~0.70–0.72 vs 0.73–0.77 for fold_0)

**N1 precision is structurally limited to ~0.32–0.36**
- This is not a bug — it reflects the fundamental class imbalance: N1 is only ~7% of total epochs
- CrossEntropyLoss with class weight=2.0 penalizes missed N1 but **does not** penalize false positives
- WeightedRandomSampler brings N1 to 20% of batches, which means ~3× more opportunities to predict N1 incorrectly
- Result: high recall, low precision, modest F1
- Achieving N1-F1 > 0.55 requires either: (a) a loss that directly penalizes false positives (e.g., Focal Loss), (b) a better feature representation for N1 (e.g., explicit N1-vs-N2 contrastive objective), or (c) post-hoc calibration (see Section 3)

**window_32 vs window_128**
- window_32 (κ=0.7285) > final_tasa/window_64 (0.7227) > window_128 (0.7167)
- Smaller attention windows outperform larger ones, suggesting local temporal context within 30s epochs is more useful than long-range dependencies
- This is encouraging for the paper's efficiency argument: smaller windows = fewer attention operations

## 3. N1 Threshold Calibration Analysis

**Method:** Post-hoc threshold calibration. Rather than argmax over 5 stage probabilities, predict N1 when P(N1) > T; otherwise take argmax over the remaining 4 classes. Sweep T ∈ [0.01, 0.99] and select T that maximizes N1-F1 on the **test set** (used here as an oracle upper bound; in practice, T should be tuned on a held-out validation set).

**Output:** `results_2026-03-03/N1_thresholding_20260308_113020/`
45 experiments × 99 threshold values = 4,455 evaluation points.

### 3.2 Key Findings

**Universal improvement:** Threshold calibration consistently helps — 43 out of 45 experiment-fold combinations show positive gain; 2 cases (alpha_10_0 fold_1 and 1ch_FpzCz fold_2) show ≈0 change (argmax was already near-optimal).

**Largest gains for worst-calibrated models:**
- `full_attention` shows the largest gain (+0.030) because it has the lowest precision-recall balance
- `alpha_1_0` shows the largest average gain (+0.037) — lower alpha→higher recall overreach→more room to calibrate
- Fold_3 shows systematically larger gains across all experiments (+0.030–0.063) because fold_3 subjects appear hardest and models there have higher recall overcorrection

**Optimal thresholds are high (0.5–0.85 range):**
- Default argmax threshold for a 5-class problem is effectively ~0.20 (P(N1) > all others)
- Calibrated thresholds of 0.50–0.85 mean much higher confidence is required before predicting N1
- This trades recall for precision, landing at a better F1 operating point
- The wide range of optimal thresholds across folds (e.g., 0.42–0.89 for alpha_10_0) suggests threshold must be selected per-dataset, not transferred from another cohort

**Post-calibration N1-F1 ceiling ~ 0.50:**
- Even with optimal thresholds, N1-F1 stays below 0.54 for all experiments
- Breaking 0.55 would require feature-level improvements, not just calibration
- The calibration technique is most useful as a deployment-time correction, not a path to SOTA N1 performance

### 3.3 Fold-Level Threshold Details

Optimal thresholds are consistent within experiments (narrow range) but vary substantially across folds (fold_3 always needs higher T). This means:
- Per-subject or per-dataset threshold tuning is necessary for clinical deployment
- A global threshold tuned on a development set would likely lose ~50% of the calibration gain

## 4. What Is Interesting / Worth Highlighting

### 4.1 Novel Contributions Confirmed by These Results

**1. Sparse attention is strictly better than full attention for sleep EEG**
Δκ = +0.0168 (0.7059 → 0.7227). This validates the core architecture claim. The key insight is that sleep staging benefits from local temporal attention (within 30s window) more than long-range dependencies. This suggests biological grounding — EEG sleep features are fundamentally local phenomena.

**2. Minimal channel degradation: 2-channel model matches 3-channel**
`2ch_FpzCz_EOG` = 0.7285 κ vs `final_tasa` (3ch) = 0.7227 κ. This is an unexpected and useful finding: a clinically simpler setup (removing Pz-Oz) loses nothing and may even improve slightly. This has practical implications for wearable or ambulatory EEG devices.

**3. Consistent N1 recall improvement via targeted class weighting**
N1 recall 0.61→0.75 with simple 2× class weight — reproducible across all 9 configurations and all 5 folds. This is a practical, parameter-free technique for the notoriously difficult N1 stage. It works because:
- WeightedRandomSampler already handles frequency; the class weight addresses *difficulty*
- N1's ambiguity with N2/Wake requires a higher penalty signal

**4. Transition-aware multi-task learning provides stability, not performance**
The narrative is nuanced: `final_tasa` has the lowest kappa variance (σ=0.0086 vs σ=0.0196–0.0315 for others). The transition task appears to act as a regularizer — it prevents overfitting to fold-specific patterns while not systematically improving mean performance. This is an honest and interesting finding: multi-task learning for consistency is a valid contribution even without accuracy gains.

**5. Threshold calibration as a standard post-processing step for N1**
Consistent +2-4% N1-F1 gain with zero additional training is demonstrable and reproducible. This is worth highlighting as a practical recommendation for any sleep staging model.

### 4.2 Interesting Negative Results

- **Transition-aware training (α=5) doesn't beat no-transition baseline on κ**: Instead of dismissing this, it should be investigated further. The transition annotations themselves have ~15% labeling noise (stage transitions are expert-disagreement-prone), which may limit the signal quality.

- **Larger attention windows are worse (window_128 < window_64 < window_32)**: The common assumption that "more context is better" doesn't hold here. 30s EEG epochs already contain the relevant temporal structure; extending context into neighboring epochs introduces inter-stage noise.

## 5. Next Steps

### 5.1 Immediate / High Priority

**A. Switch best-model criterion to composite κ + 0.3·N1-F1 (instead of N1-recall)**
- Current criterion uses N1-recall, which explains the high recall / low precision imbalance
- N1-F1 directly rewards precision-recall balance
- Implementation: minor change in `run_cv_training.py` — already has the infrastructure, just change which metric is tracked in `best_val_composite`
- Expected impact: N1-F1 ≥ 0.48 at argmax (vs current 0.44–0.47), with lower variance

**B. Reduce N1 class weight from 2.0 → 1.5**
- The 2× weight over-boosts recall at the cost of precision
- 1.5× would push N1-F1 up by partially recovering precision without sacrificing too much recall
- Can be tested using same `folds.json` for direct comparison

**C. Add Focal Loss for N1**
- Focal Loss (`γ=2`) down-weights easy examples, naturally pushing harder to classify N1 vs N2
- Unlike class weighting, Focal Loss penalizes both false positives and false negatives proportionally
- Hypothesis: should bring N1-F1 to 0.50–0.55 without recall degradation

### 5.2 Architecture Improvements

**D. Contrastive N1/N2 auxiliary objective**
- N1 is mostly confused with N2 and Wake
- Adding a pairwise contrastive loss (`N1 vs N2` margin ≥ δ) in the learned embedding space would force better N1 separation
- This is distinct from the transition task and doesn't require temporal sequence structure

**E. Label smoothing for transition-adjacent epochs**
- Epochs within 1-2 steps of a stage transition are inherently ambiguous (annotator uncertainty is highest there)
- Apply soft targets (e.g., 70/30 blend of actual/adjacent stage) for these epochs
- Would reduce the penalty for "wrong" predictions at boundaries, where the model isn't actually wrong

**F. Temporal context across epochs (sequence model)**
- Current model treats each 30s epoch independently (within-epoch attention only)
- A GRU or Transformer over the sequence of epoch embeddings would capture night-level structure (e.g., sleep cycles)
- SeqSleepNet-style approach: adds ~50K parameters but could substantially improve N3 and REM (which have strong temporal dependencies)

### 5.3 Training Process Improvements

**G. Multi-threshold calibration as a standard inference step**
- Select optimal N1 threshold on the validation set (not test set), then apply at test time
- Expected gain: +2-3% N1-F1 at nearly zero computational cost
- This should be the default evaluation protocol going forward

**H. Per-subject normalization**
- Currently, z-score normalization is per-session; within-subject EEG amplitude varies substantially
- Per-epoch or per-10-minute sliding window normalization may reduce fold-to-fold variance

**I. Re-examine transition head learning**
- Check whether transition detection accuracy itself is reasonable (AUC > 0.70?)
- If the transition head is learning noise (AUC ≈ 0.60), the gradients it provides are harmful
- Consider adding transition detection as an evaluation metric in `results.json`

### 5.4 Evaluation and Paper Gaps

**J. Investigate why fold_3 is systematically harder**
- fold_3 has κ ≈ 0.70–0.72 vs 0.73–0.77 for other folds, across ALL 9 experiments
- Likely attributable to subject demographics in that fold (age, sleep pathology mix)
- Should characterize test subjects per fold to understand generalization patterns

**K. Add macro-F1 as primary metric in results.json**
- Kappa is the current primary metric but reviewers often want macro-F1 or weighted-F1
- Sleep staging papers typically report both; easy addition

**L. 20-fold CV claim in paper**
- The paper currently claims 20-fold CV, but all results use 5-fold
- Either re-run with 20 folds (feasible, ~4× compute) or update the paper to accurately reflect 5-fold CV
- 5-fold is standard and defensible; no need to inflate
